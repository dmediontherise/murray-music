## Part I: Extended Chords (Color)

If triads are primary colors, **Extensions** are the shading and texture. We stack more 3rds on top of the 7th to reach the 9th, 11th, and 13th.

### 1.1 Major 7th Chords
**Formula: 1 - 3 - 5 - 7**
Imagine a Major triad, but you add a note that is just one half-step below the octave. This adds a soft, fuzzy layer of nostalgia. It is the core sound of Lo-Fi Hip Hop and Jazz Ballads.

### 1.2 Dominant 7th Chords
**Formula: 1 - 3 - 5 - b7**
Take a Major triad, but add a *flatted* 7th. This creates a "Tritone" interval inside the chord (between the 3rd and the b7). This internal friction makes the chord sound bluesy and edgy. It is the driving force of almost all chord progressionsâ€”it wants desperately to push you home to the Tonic.

### 1.3 Minor 7th Chords
**Formula: 1 - b3 - 5 - b7**
A Minor triad with a flat 7. This takes the "sadness" of a minor chord and makes it sound "mellow" or "sophisticated" instead. It is the main chord used in Deep House and Neo-Soul.

### 1.6 Extensions: The 9th
Adding the 9th creates a rich, sophisticated sound. Dom9 chords are the staple of James Brown Funk. Maj9 chords are the staple of Neo-Soul.

## Part II: Functional Harmony

### 2.4 Secondary Dominants
Once you understand that V7 pulls to I, you can "hack" the system. You can treat *any* chord in your progression as a temporary "I" chord, and precede it with its own "V7".

For example, in C Major, the ii chord is Dm. If you want to emphasize Dm, you can play the V7 of Dm (which is A7) right before it. This is called a Secondary Dominant.

> **Listen For:** The bridge of "Yesterday" by The Beatles. The chord progression goes Em -> A7 -> Dm. The A7 is the Secondary Dominant pointing to Dm.

### 2.5 Tritone Substitution
This is the "Secret Handshake" of Jazz musicians. It allows you to replace a Dominant 7th chord with another Dominant 7th chord exactly a **Tritone (6 semitones) away**.

Why does it work? Because they share the same Tritone! In G7, the tritone is B & F. In Db7, the tritone is F & Cb (B). Since the "active ingredients" are the same, they perform the same function, but the Db7 allows for a cool chromatic bass line (D -> Db -> C).

## Part III: The Modes (Flavors)

Modes are simply the Major Scale starting from a different note. If you play the C Major scale but start and end on D, you are playing **D Dorian**. The notes are the same, but the "center of gravity" shifts, creating a totally different mood.

### 3.2 Dorian (The Jazz Minor)
Built on the 2nd degree. It has a natural 6th, making it brighter than standard minor. It is the sound of "So What" by Miles Davis and "Oye Como Va".

### 3.3 Phrygian (The Exotic Minor)
Built on the 3rd degree. It features a b2. It sounds Spanish, heavy, or metal.

### 3.4 Lydian (The Dream Mode)
Built on the 4th degree. It features a #4. It sounds floaty, magical, and Sci-Fi. It is the sound of *The Simpsons* theme and many John Williams scores (E.T.).

### 3.5 Mixolydian (The Rock Major)
Built on the 5th degree. It features a b7. It sounds like a Major scale with a bluesy edge. Used by The Beatles ("Norwegian Wood") and AC/DC.

## Part IV: Modern & Jazz Concepts

### 4.1 Shell Voicings
Amateurs stack chords 1-3-5-7. Pros use **Voicings**. A Shell Voicing strips the chord down to its absolute skeleton. You play only the **Root, 3rd, and 7th**. This frees up your other fingers to play extensions or melody. Often, the Root is even dropped (Rootless Voicings) because the bass player is already playing it.

### 4.3 Quartal Harmony (So What)
Instead of stacking notes in 3rds (Tertain harmony), we stack them in **4ths**. This creates a very modern, ambiguous sound. It was popularized by McCoy Tyner and Bill Evans.

### 4.5 Altered Dominants
To create maximum tension, we alter the extensions. The most famous example is the **Sharp 9 (#9)**. It clashes against the major 3rd, creating a bluesy "growl" famously used by Jimi Hendrix (the "Hendrix Chord").

### 4.8 Bebop Enclosures
In Bebop, you rarely play a target note directly. You "encircle" it first. You might play the note *above* it, then the note *below* it, and *then* the target note. This creates a sense of momentum and "landing."
